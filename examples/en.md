## My Story

### The Starting Point: Just Like Everyone Else (September 2024 — June 2025)

In September 2024, I enrolled in the Management program at Fudan University. Like most of my classmates, I started using AI — Doubao, DeepSeek — but for a very narrow purpose: homework, especially math. To me, AI was just a slightly smarter search engine that could help me solve problems.

Looking back, I actually encountered AI tool chains during my freshman year. That semester, I took an ecology course that required us to build workflows on Coze. I built one, submitted it, and my internal reaction was "this is nothing special." I didn't realize the productivity revolution behind workflows, nor did I have any interest in exploring further. Throughout my entire freshman year, my attitude toward AI could be summed up in one word: indifferent.

Now I realize I have a tendency to hold onto preconceptions and resist new things. This pattern would repeat itself throughout my journey.

### The Turning Point: Tencent Product Manager Training Camp (July 2025)

In July 2025, I attended the Tencent Product Manager Training Camp. To be honest, when I signed up, I didn't have a deep understanding of what a "product manager" does — it was a fairly impulsive decision. But this camp completely changed my perception of AI.

What changed me wasn't the curriculum itself — it was the people. The camp had a QQ channel where all participants shared what they were working on and what they'd learned. What struck me most was that some students chose to build games as their projects — they coded the games themselves using AI and shared the links for everyone to try. I played them, thought they were impressive, and messaged them to exchange contacts. Through those conversations, I learned they were using an approach called "vibe coding" — no professional programming background needed, just AI assistance to build products. And these people were all closely following AI frontiers and product trends.

I had an idea: why not bring all these talented people together? So I created a community and actively invited the most capable people from the camp to join. Over time, the group gathered quite a few experts who shared interesting AI products, valuable AI communities, and cutting-edge information. This community now has about 200 members.

It was also through this community that I learned about Baidu Miaoda, a vibe coding platform, and their no-code hackathon. Without building this community, I almost certainly wouldn't have known about these opportunities.

Looking back, I noticed a pattern: what triggered me was never the tool itself, but seeing real people use tools to build real things. Coze workflows didn't move me because I only saw a course assignment. But seeing peers use vibe coding to build playable games — that struck a chord.

### First Real-World Attempt: Baidu Miaoda Hackathon (September 2025)

In September 2025, I participated in Baidu Miaoda's no-code hackathon. This was my first hands-on vibe coding project.

The beauty of hackathons is that as long as you have an idea, you can build it — the barrier to entry is very low. I built multiple projects, and two of them won awards.

The first was a **Physics-Chemistry-Biology Lesson Prep Assistant** for middle and high school teachers. This idea didn't come from thin air — I asked friends around me, "What scenario has a major pain point but no good solution?" One friend mentioned teachers' lesson preparation: when introducing new topics in physics, chemistry, or biology, teachers need engaging opening segments, but existing materials use outdated examples. Teachers wanted to incorporate cutting-edge developments and latest research into their lesson openings, but finding these materials themselves took too much time. I felt this was a genuine need: clear target audience, strong willingness to use, and AI could genuinely solve it. So I built the product. It won an Excellence Award at the Shanghai offline hackathon.

The second was a **City Walk Smart Route Planner**. Users input their starting point and destination, and the backend intelligently plans a route with Baidu Maps navigation, marking interesting food, drinks, and activities along the way. I put a lot of effort into the UI — I specifically chose a cherry blossom photo as the background, and the entire page used a cherry blossom pink color scheme for a visually comfortable experience. This project won a National Outstanding Award.

After these two projects, I summarized three key elements: first, find a novel but practical entry point where the need is genuine; second, pay special attention to UI/UX design — looking good and being usable directly affects first impressions from judges and users; third, think through the business logic — products ultimately need to achieve commercial viability.

But the most noteworthy point is this: neither project idea was my own — both came from asking friends and conducting user research. This pattern would repeat itself, and I sometimes felt anxious about lacking creativity. But I gradually came to terms with it — a product manager's core competency isn't being an inventor; it's the ability to extract the most worthwhile needs from users and the market, then execute on them.

### Second Hackathon: Fudan School of Management × Douyin Training Camp (November 2025)

In November 2025, I participated in a training camp jointly organized by Fudan's School of Management and Douyin — my second vibe coding project.

We built a **Cross-Cultural Gift-Giving Assistant**: users input their cultural background and the recipient's cultural background, and the product provides targeted gift suggestions and taboos. For example, cultural differences between Han and ethnic minorities, or between Chinese and foreign cultures. This project won the Most Creative Award. The judges' feedback was that the idea was excellent, but gift-giving involves many subtle social nuances that are difficult to systematically feed to AI without a very comprehensive rule system.

Frankly, I don't think I made any significant progress this time. The reason is straightforward: I still used Baidu Miaoda, exactly the same tool as the previous hackathon. Two months had passed, and I hadn't tried any new tools or explored any new tech stack. I realized I have a tendency toward "staying in my comfort zone" when it comes to tools — once I'm comfortable with one, I'm reluctant to try others.

### Woken Up by an Interviewer's Question (December 2025 — February 2026)

After the training camp, winter break arrived and I started sending out resumes. My direction was clear: I only wanted AI product intern positions, and every company I applied to was hiring for AI product roles.

One interview stands out in my memory. It was a CEO interview at a company, and I was rejected within the first 10 minutes. The reason: he asked me, "Why do you only use domestic AI tools and not international ones?" I couldn't answer. The truth was, using international AI tools required a VPN, and I was quite conservative about spending money — I'd never bought a VPN or any AI tool subscription.

This rejection hit me hard. It directly exposed a problem: my AI tool horizon was limited to the domestic ecosystem, and I hadn't even realized this was a limitation.

After that, I interviewed with a startup and became an AI product intern. The company made an AI hardware product for the North American market — a smart photo frame targeting elderly people living alone and their adult children (known in the US as the "sandwich generation," caring for both aging parents and young children). The product had two sides: a children's app and an elderly person's photo frame. Children could upload photos, and AI would automatically convert static images into animated clips or short videos with voice narration explaining the content. On the elderly side, they could continue interacting with AI to ask about the photos. The frame also had a long-term companionship feature: morning greetings, weather and news broadcasts, evening goodnights, and on-demand AI conversation. The children's app could view all daily AI conversation records to understand the elderly person's status.

My job was designing the children's app's interaction and pages, planning the feature layout and onboarding flow, and designing AI prompts for each scenario on the elderly side. For example, the morning wake-up feature: should AI abruptly say "good morning," or should there be a gentle music transition from soft to loud before speaking? Should the frame display a notification telling the elderly person "AI is about to speak" to avoid startling them? Every detail needed careful consideration.

Although I only worked there for about two weeks, this experience taught me several important things. First, product design can start with coarse granularity — figure out the main features first, and dive into interaction details during development. Second, the biggest difference between AI products and regular products is the prompts — they need to be excellent, you need to understand AI's capability boundaries, and you need to know what outputs to expect. And since we can't predict what users will say, we need to test AI performance across various scenarios to ensure stable results. Third, the CEO told me something that left a deep impression: the most important thing is taste. Experience more art, study good products, draw inspiration from different fields, and broaden your horizons.

But this internship only lasted two weeks. On one hand, the CEO wasn't satisfied with me; on the other hand, I didn't take it seriously myself — I felt that as just an intern, I shouldn't be expected to invest that much time and energy, and I was resistant to overtime. Looking back now, that attitude was problematic.

However, because of this internship, I finally took the step of getting a VPN. I started using various international AI tools: ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, Manus, Google AI Studio, Google Stitch, Loveart, and more. My tool horizon instantly expanded from domestic to global. This was probably the biggest incidental gain from those two weeks.

One more thing worth recording from winter break: two friends and I wanted to expand our AI community operations. We started with five people, but two dropped out, and the expansion plan was shelved. But the three of us developed a deep friendship and launched a public account dedicated to sharing AI news, commentary, and product insights. Although the community didn't work out, the public account became an outlet for sustained output and accumulation.

### Entering a Platform-Level Project: Sandpile (March 2026 — Present)

In March 2026, I joined Sandpile. Sandpile is an Agent integration network — essentially a platform where different people's Agents can connect, interact, and transact. Different developers connect their Agents to Sandpile, each with their own skills, and others can invoke them, creating transactions between Agents.

I joined before Sandpile was formally established, making me an original member. Although someone else initiated the project, the CEO is quite open-minded, and the team atmosphere feels more like a group of people starting a company together. My roles span several dimensions: product design, information gathering and analysis, thinking about AI's future development direction and where Sandpile should head, and building connections with universities — because Sandpile needs to onboard a large number of Agents and developers, and young university students are a great entry point.

The first thing we did was design a Financial Agent. As platform creators, we needed to create an initial batch of Agents with unique skills that others couldn't easily replicate, to attract others to use them. We chose the financial domain because the team was already familiar with it, and besides open-source data sources, we paid for other data APIs, creating a data moat.

We designed an entire multi-Agent collaboration workflow: when a user enters a query (e.g., "look up ByteDance for me"), the backend Agent first decomposes the task into multiple steps, each corresponding to required skills and which Agents are needed. Multiple qualified Agents compete for the same task, the most suitable one is selected for execution, and all results are aggregated and returned to the user.

At the end of March, we participated in a global developer conference to showcase Sandpile's initial results and attract the first batch of seed users. I was there on-site for promotion. After that, we started planning what categories of Agents the platform needed, currently targeting what Agents hackathon participants and indie developers need, and what Agents VC investors need.

The university outreach direction has been set, but honestly, it hasn't been truly executed yet — I'm just finding AI entrepreneurs and AI bloggers at Fudan, without a systematic推进 plan.

### The Present: Multiple Parallel Tracks (April — May 2026)

While working on Sandpile, I met the head of a film and television company at the developer conference in late March. Now I'm interning there, helping optimize AI workflows: automating script translation, video editing, meeting interpretation, PPT template creation — all done by AI. In this role, I'm using Codex and Claude Code, a significant change from only using Baidu Miaoda before.

In April 2026, I participated in a vibe coding workshop jointly organized by Fudan's School of Management and Garena, where I built a pixel detective game. After the workshop, I discussed an internship opportunity with Garena and received an interview invitation, but I didn't pass.

I did a very thorough post-mortem on this failed interview. The surface reason was inadequate preparation: I already had two internship experiences, but when the interviewer asked about them, I had no idea how to organize my thoughts. My answers lacked logic and were very brief for every question. I hadn't even prepared my opening self-introduction in advance — I completely winged it. The interviewer therefore found me wooden and passive, concluding that I'd be the type who "only does what they're told" at work.

The deeper reason was that I wasn't interested in Garena's AI social product from the start, feeling it was inferior to another product called Second Me. This attitude was detected by the interviewer. Looking back, my reaction was problematic — their product was already fully developed and quite mature; the fact that it was launched meant it had gone through extensive discussion and refinement, and it must have its strengths. As a product manager, I should have tried to discover a product's highlights and differentiators first, rather than immediately comparing it to another product in my mind and dismissing it.

The most fundamental reason, I think, was my daily routine. I've never developed good sleep and exercise habits, and poor physical condition directly affects emotional stability, causing me to underperform in high-stakes scenarios like interviews that require focus and enthusiasm.

It's now May 2026. I'm advancing on two tracks simultaneously: Sandpile and the film company. Recently, I also met a classmate who's an AI product intern at StepFun. She felt Fudan lacked an atmosphere for AI enthusiasts to connect, so she organized an AI coffee chat event and invited me to participate, starting next week. Additionally, my university friends are actually quite AI-embracing — we chat about it in class, and I've learned many techniques from them. Honestly, some of them are as proficient with AI as I am, or even more so.
